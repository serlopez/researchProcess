the research process

foreword: what is this and what it is for

the purpose of this class is to teach higher education students, anyone else interested, and you, hopefully, how to do library research.

this class is designed to be both self taught, taught online, or in person, both synchronously and asynchronously.

the class consists of seven small chapters plus appendices on relevant topics.

each chapter is divided as follows:

  • title
  • objective: what I expect you to know at the end of each chapter.
  • vocabulary: words or terms key to understanding the chapter.
  • the chapter’s main content.
  • conclusion: the main ideas of the chapter.
  • activities: optional but good to reinforce your understanding.
  • auto-evaluation: a small exercise, often a small quiz, to review what you learned.

depending on your or your professor’s needs, you will go over one or more chapters. however, a basic understanding of the whole class will prove very useful.

let's go.

chapter 0: the research process

objective: the student will identify the research process as well as its steps.

vocabulary: research, process.

writing an essay, composing a speech, or putting together a presentation can all be thought of as processes.

the research process, as the name indicates, is the process of doing research.

when it comes to library research, the research process consists of:

  • knowing the assingment's specifics and deadlines
  • choosing a topic
  • finding background information in reference publications
  • looking for specific information using library resources and the web
  • organizing foudn information in a logical manner
  • citing, formatting, and packaging for delivery

conclusion: many class assignments require students to do library research. the research process makes library research more efficient.

the first step in the research process is to detemine the projects's specifications and expectations.

familiarizing yourself with the project before getting to work on it is generally a good idea. expectations.

    things you should know about your project.

  • overall subject: choice or theme?

Chapter 2: subject, topic and keywords.

objective: the student will derive a topic of adequate scope, given an umbrella subject, and derive appropriate keywords to begin research using the method described below.

vocabulary: topic, subject, keyword, scope, reliable.

the next step in the research process is to propose a topic from a given, general subject, and develop keywords.

determine the general subject of your project.

scope. too narrow? not enough information, results in wasted time doing hard research.
too broad? too much information, results in generic, boring papers in which no one learned anything.

to help you narrow your project to an adequate topic, ask yourself:

  • how much you know about the topic
  • how much you don't know about the topic
  • if what you know about the topic is reliable

finally, pick a topic you are curious about or find interesting.

conclusion: narrowing a project’s scope to an adequate topic can be the hardest part of research. make sure you spend enough time working on it.
