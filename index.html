the research process

foreword: what is this and what it is for

the purpose of this class is to teach higher education students, anyone else interested, and you, hopefully, how to do library research.

this class is designed to be both self taught, taught online, or in person, both synchronously and asynchronously.

the class consists of seven small chapters plus appendices on relevant topics.

each chapter is divided as follows:

  • title
  • objective: what I expect you to know at the end of each chapter.
  • vocabulary: words or terms key to understanding the chapter.
  • the chapter’s main content.
  • conclusion: the main ideas of the chapter.
  • activities: optional but good to reinforce your understanding.
  • auto-evaluation: a small exercise, often a small quiz, to review what you learned.

depending on your or your professor’s needs, you will go over one or more chapters. however, a basic understanding of the whole class will prove very useful.

let's go.

chapter 0: the research process

objective: the student will identify the research process as well as its steps.

vocabulary: research, process.

writing an essay, composing a speech, or putting together a presentation can all be thought of as processes.

the research process, as the name indicates, is the process of doing research.

when it comes to library research, the research process consists of the following steps:

  • knowing the assingment's specifics and deadlines
  • choosing a topic
  • finding background information in reference publications
  • looking for specific information using library resources and the web
  • organizing foudn information in a logical manner
  • citing, formatting, and packaging for delivery

conclusion: many class assignments require students to do library research. the research process makes library research more efficient.

discuss or think about the role the research process has in critical thinking.

Chapter 2: subject, topic and keywords.

objective: the student will derive a topic of adequate scope, given an umbrella subject, and derive appropriate keywords to begin research using the method described below.

vocabulary: topic, subject, keyword, scope, reliable.

the next step in the research process is to establish a topic from a given, general subject, and develop keywords.

first, determine the general subject of your project. talk to your professor.

scope. too narrow, specific? not enough information, results in wasted time doing hard research.
too broad, vague? too much information, results in generic, boring papers in which no one learned anything.

examples:

i want to write about the effects plastic pollution, specifically disposable coffee brew cups, has on the daily life of the piripkura tribe in the brazilian amazon.

to help you narrow your project to an adequate topic, first phrase it as a question.

then ask yourself:

  • how much you know about the topic
  • how much you don't know about the topic
  • if what you know about the topic is reliable

finally, pick a topic you are curious about or find interesting.

chapter 3: finding background information.

objective: the student will learn how to use dictionaries, encyclopedia and reputable online reference resources to obtain basic facts and the proper vocabulary.

vocabulary: reference, encyclopedia, dictionary, source

once you have a topic to research, the next step is to find background information to help you research said topic.

by background information, we mean the basic, essential, general information and core concepts.

in addition to general information about your topic, you will want to learn the specific teminology, key words, and special vocabulary.

Use encyclopedias, dictionaries, and wikipedia, to find background information.

yes, use wikipedia, just don't tell me you did.

allow me to explain.

wikipedia is a great place to begin research, as long as you keep in mind that wikipedia is a place, not a source. we don’t cite places, we cite sources. use wikipedia’s reference list to find the source, and cite the source.

none of your statements should ever begin with "according to wikipedia,..." or "wikipedia says...". if you find yourself doing this, stop and read the paragraph above.

become familiar with the core concepts, special vocabulary, and relevant people in your topic.

ask your instructor or librarian where you can find encyclopedias, dictionaries, or other reference materials relevant to your project.

conclusion: encyclopedias, dictionaries, and wikipedia are excellent sources to begin your research by finding background information and specific keywords/terms.

activity: practice finding the list of references for wikipedia pages of your choice. make sure you can trace information back to the source and there is actually a source (404’s, broken links).

chapter 4: using library resources to find quality information.

objective: the student will learn how to use the library catalog and library databases.

vocabulary: library catalog, databases, monograph, article, journal, database, abstract.

searching for books in the libray catalog

encyclopedias, dictionaries, and wikipedia provide background information to begin research, introduce you to the topic, and provide useful keywords and key terms.

however, this information is usually not enough. for more substantial information, we need to use the resources found in your library.

through your college (or public library) you will find books using the library catalog and academic quality articles using the library’s electronic databases.

books are the best choice when looking for in-depth information about a topic. books usually talk about one thing for a very long time.

when looking for books, start with a broad subject.

books are found in the library using the library catalog.

to find books in the library catalog:

  • locate the library catalog
  • type your keyword(s) and click "search"

search will take you to the library catalog, a list of the items in the library matching the search terms.

observe that you will find, in addition to books in print, ebooks, streaming video, dvd, even some journal articles. however, we use the library catalog when we are looking for books and ebooks in general.

if you are looking for eBooks, select ebooks under resource type. likewise for print books, or any other format.

click on the title of the book of your choice. notice the detailed information about the book, its location and useful tools.

first, notice title, author, publication date and summary.

now look at the book’s availability: here you can find out if the book is available and where in the shelves it’s located. if it’s an ebook, it's usually just a matter of following links.

lastly, notice the tools field. here you will find the correct citation in the style of your choice, for when the time comes to cite your sources.

if we don’t have the book you need and another library has it and it’s willing to lend it, we can get the book for you by summit or interlibrary loan (ill).

searching for articles in library databases

while books talk about only one thing, in depth, for a very long time, journal articles found in databases usually talk about many, very specific things, for a short period of time.

make sure you know the difference between database, journal, and article, as well as their relationship.

library { databases { journal { articles } } }.

to find journal articles from the library:

  • locate the library research link
  • at the library research page, click the databases link

here you will find the find articles page. from here you can find databases either by name through the a-z or by subject.

examples of the databases you will find the most useful are academic search complete, popular magazines, and opposing viewpoints.

for the most part, databases’ use and structure are very similar:

click the database's link. if you see a page asking you for your mhcc id number and mymhcc password, please have it ready (not an issue if you do this from campus).

if everything goes as expected, you will see the database’ initial search page. type your keywords, use boolean logic if necessary, select articles in full text, peer-reviewed if the professor specified it, date of publication limits if desired, and press search.

if your search is successful, the results page will show a list of articles relevant to your keywords. select an article by clicking its title.

here you will find more information about the article, including the title, authors, journal name, and, most importantly, the abstract. the abstract is your friend!

if you think this article is good for your project, access is by clicking the full text link.

lastly, here you can print the article, save it to a flash drive, even the database will give you a folder to save your research. the easiest way is to have the database email you the articles.

here is where you will find the correct citation, in the style of your choice, copy and paste the citation. the database can also send you the article’s citation by email, along with the article.

you may be able to find articles easier through google scholar. however, accessing databases using scholar is like trying to get in using the back door. if you try to download through google scholar, you will likely get a message asking who the hell are you and demanding $30. we will talk about searching the web in more detail below.

conclusion: use library resources to find books, ebooks and articles from journal databases.

chapter 5: searching for information on the web.

objective: the student will use the web and the site: command to search for information.

vocabulary: internet, web, domain.

while library resources may be more than enough for your project, you may have to go to the web to get the information you need.

besides, since we use it everyday, we might as well know how to use it properly.

domain: the .something ending of each website's name. each website has its domain.

websites can be classified by domains, depending on their origin, function, content, and/or purpose.

  • .com (personal or commercial)
  • .net (personal or commercial. less popular)
  • .org (non-for-profit, charitable organizations)
  • .edu (colleges and universities, research institutions)
  • .gov (u. s. government)
  • .mil (u. s. military)
  • .int (international treaty organizations)

knowing about domains comes in handy because we can use them to get better quality information from the internet by searching within a specific domain.

to search the internet within a specific domain, we use the site: command.

how it works:

  • open your browser.
  • in google search, type your keyword or question.
  • type site:domain or website after or before your question.

not only it works for domains, it allows you to fiind information within a single website's url.

conclusion: the site: command is a powerful tool we can use to search the web for quality information for our project.

activity: find information on the web coming from .com, .org, .edu, and .gov websites. Compare results and think on how this feature can help you do web research.
